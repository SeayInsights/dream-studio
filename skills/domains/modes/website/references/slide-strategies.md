# Slide Strategies — Duarte Sparkline Methodology

This reference governs how to structure a presentation's narrative arc and select the right slide type for each moment. Read this before building any deck. It is a decision guide, not a textbook.

---

## The Sparkline Concept

Every great presentation oscillates between two states:

```
WHAT IS ──────╮        ╭─────── WHAT IS ──────╮        ╭─── NEW BLISS
              ╰──────╯                         ╰──────╯
          WHAT COULD BE                    WHAT COULD BE
```

- **What Is** — current reality, the problem, the status quo
- **What Could Be** — the vision, the opportunity, the better future
- **New Bliss** — the destination audience reaches after they act

The gap between What Is and What Could Be creates emotional tension. The job of the deck is to widen that gap until the audience feels compelled to cross it.

---

## Narrative Arc Structure

### Act 1: The Beginning (10-15% of slides)
- **Hook** — Start with something unexpected: a jarring statistic, a short story, or a sharp question
- **What Is** — Establish current reality; give the audience a shared starting point
- **Goal**: Create common ground before challenging it

### Act 2: The Middle (70-80% of slides)
- Alternate between **What Is** and **What Could Be**
- Each contrast raises the stakes — make the gap feel larger each cycle
- Back every "What Could Be" claim with evidence or a story
- Use data to prove "What Is" is unsustainable; use vision to make "What Could Be" feel reachable

### Act 3: The End (10-15% of slides)
- **New Bliss** — Paint the picture of life after the audience acts
- **Call to Action** — One specific, actionable, time-bound ask
- **Goal**: Send the audience out with energy and a clear next step

## Slide Type Selection Guide

| Moment in Arc | Recommended Slide Type | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Opening hook | `data-viz` or `quote` | Data surprises; quotes resonate emotionally |
| "What Is" | `content` or `data-viz` | Facts about current state need grounding |
| "What Could Be" | `image` or `comparison` | Vision needs visual impact, not bullet points |
| Contrast point | `comparison` | Side-by-side makes the gap vivid and undeniable |
| Evidence | `data-viz` | Numbers build credibility |
| Story moment | `image` or `quote` | Stories need visual space, not competing text |
| Section transition | `section` | Gives the audience a mental reset |
| "New Bliss" | `image` | Paint the picture — let the visual do the work |
| Call to Action | `cta` | Clear, prominent, nothing competing for attention |

---

## Pacing Rules

| Talk length | Slide count | Structure |
|---|---|---|
| 5 minutes | 5–8 slides | Hook → 2 contrasts → CTA |
| 10 minutes | 8–15 slides | Hook → 3–4 contrasts → evidence → CTA |
| 20 minutes | 15–25 slides | Hook → 5–6 contrasts → multiple evidence points → CTA |

**Rule of thumb**: ~1 slide per minute. Never more than 1.5 slides per minute. Rushing means the deck has too many slides or the wrong slide types.

---

## Content Density Rules

These are hard limits. Exceeding them signals the content belongs in a document, not a presentation.

| Slide Type | Limit |
|---|---|
| Title / Section | 1–5 words |
| Content | Max 5 bullets × 25 words each |
| Data-viz | 1 chart + 1 takeaway sentence |
| Quote | Max 30 words |
| Image | Max 10 words overlay |
| CTA | 1 action + 1 deadline |

---

## Common Anti-Patterns

1. **Data dump** — Charts without a narrative thread. Data should support the arc, not replace it.
2. **Wall of text** — Reading slides aloud kills engagement. If it needs to be read, it's a document.
3. **No contrast** — All "What Is" with no vision = boring. All "What Could Be" with no grounding = unbelievable. Both fail.
4. **Weak close** — Ending on a Q&A slide or "Thank You" discards the CTA momentum. Always close on the action.
5. **Feature list** — Listing capabilities instead of telling benefit stories. Audiences buy outcomes, not features.
6. **Apologizing** — "I know this is hard to read" means redesign the slide, not acknowledge it.
7. **Animation overload** — Transitions and entrance effects distract from content. Use animation only when it clarifies a concept.

## Decision Framework — Planning a Deck

Answer these four questions before selecting a single slide type:

1. **What does the audience believe NOW?**
   → This opens "What Is." Start where they are.

2. **What should they believe AFTER?**
   → This defines "What Could Be." Every contrast moment moves toward this.

3. **What evidence bridges the gap?**
   → These become your `data-viz` slides. Pick the 2-3 most compelling facts.

4. **What is the ONE thing you want them to DO?**
   → This is your CTA. One ask, one deadline, no alternatives.

If you cannot answer all four, the deck is not ready to build. Go back to the brief.

---

## Quick Reference — Sparkline Checklist

Before finalizing a slide sequence, verify:

- [ ] Does the deck open with a hook (not a title slide of logos)?
- [ ] Is the first "What Is" grounded in the audience's current reality?
- [ ] Does each "What Could Be" slide have a visual, not just text?
- [ ] Are contrast cycles present (at least 2 oscillations in Act 2)?
- [ ] Is there at least one `data-viz` or `quote` as evidence?
- [ ] Does the final slide contain a `cta` slide type, not Q&A?
- [ ] Is total slide count within the pacing rule for the talk length?
- [ ] Does each slide stay within the content density limits?
